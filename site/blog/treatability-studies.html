Water treatability studies: what we actually look for

Treatability studies are the cheapest insurance an effluent project can buy. A well-run lab-scale trial costs a fraction of an ETP module — and we regularly see it change the technology choice in a way that saves an order of magnitude more.

What a treatability study tells you

  • Whether your candidate biological system can actually handle the load.
  • Optimal dosing for coagulation, flocculation, and polishing chemicals.
  • Sludge yield and character — a major driver of operating cost.
  • Which tertiary technology (ozone, UV, activated carbon, MBR) is worth paying for, and which is overkill for your outlet norms.

A practical study plan

For a mid-sized effluent stream, we typically run a four-week bench study: week one on characterization and jar tests, weeks two and three on biological acclimation and steady-state, week four on tertiary polishing. A well-run plan de-risks at least three major design decisions before procurement.

The three scenarios where it saves real money

  • Mixed streams where a single ETP is planned for legacy and new production — the study will show whether blending is viable or a segregated line is the better economics.
  • High-salt or high-TDS streams where MBR and RO may be sold as default choices; the study often reveals a cheaper path.
  • ZLD retrofits where the evaporator sizing can swing 20% based on accurate mass-balance from trials.

Who should run it

Treatability studies should be run by an independent consultant, not the equipment vendor. Vendor-run trials have an obvious incentive problem; independent trials give you a defensible basis for the capex decision.
