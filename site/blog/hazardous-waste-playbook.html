A hazardous waste playbook for plant managers

Most hazardous waste incidents at Indian plants are not exotic. They are ordinary mistakes stacked on top of each other — missing characterization, sloppy manifests, and informal transport. Here is the operational checklist we hand over to every new EHS officer on a Project Force engagement.

1. Characterize before you store

Every waste stream generated at your plant should map to a Schedule-I category under the Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules. If a waste does not have a clean category, sample and lab-test it — do not default to a "best guess". Inspectors can tell.

2. Segregate at the point of generation

A mixed drum is a liability. Segregate acidic, alkaline, solvent, oily, and pharma-residue waste at the point of generation, using colour-coded drums with permanent labels that survive the monsoon.

3. Storage that an auditor will sign off on

  • Covered shed with impermeable flooring and bund walls (110% of the largest drum).
  • Incompatibles physically separated — not just labelled differently.
  • Spill kit within 10m, and a drill log that proves it has been used.
  • Daily inventory reconciliation against Form 3.

4. Manifesting: the paper that protects you

Form 10 manifests are not administrative paperwork — they are legal evidence of chain of custody. Train your gatekeepers to refuse any lorry that shows up without a matching, pre-signed manifest, even if the driver insists the office "will send it later".

5. Use authorised TSDFs only

Work only with Treatment, Storage and Disposal Facilities (TSDFs) whose authorisation you have seen and verified in the current year. Keep a copy of the authorisation and insurance cover on file for every disposal partner.

6. Annual return and audit-readiness

Form 4 should be filed by June 30 each year — and the numbers should reconcile against your storage logs, manifests, and the TSDF's receipt records. If those three do not agree, fix the process before filing.
