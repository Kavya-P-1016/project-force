O&M contracts that actually keep plants compliant

Most O&M contracts are written to protect the vendor. The best ones are written to protect the plant. The difference is in three clauses — SLAs tied to outcomes, manpower blended for skill coverage, and a monthly compliance pack with teeth.

SLA: write it as outcomes, not activities

An SLA that promises "monthly maintenance of aerators" is meaningless. An SLA that promises "ETP outlet within board norms for at least 95% of monitored hours, with root-cause reporting within 24 hours of any breach" is a contract.

Manpower blend

  • One lead operator with a formal qualification and 5+ years in the specific process.
  • Two trained operators on a rotation that covers the full 24×7 cycle.
  • Support access to a chemist, mechanical technician, and electrical technician — on call if not on site.

The monthly compliance pack

Every month, the vendor should deliver a pack that includes inlet/outlet trends, consumable consumption vs benchmark, any deviations with root cause and corrective action, a list of spares replaced, and the next month's maintenance plan. If the pack does not arrive by the 5th, treat it as a defect, not a delay.

Liquidated damages vs incentive bonus

Balanced contracts combine modest penalties for breach with a small outcome-based bonus — for example, a share of savings when power or chemical consumption falls below an agreed baseline. That keeps the vendor thinking like an operator, not a caretaker.

The 90-day takeover

Whenever a plant switches O&M partners, plan a 90-day supervised handover. The first 30 days should be diagnostic, the next 30 stabilisation, and the last 30 a documented handover with a signed snag list. Every time we skip this, something breaks in the third month.
