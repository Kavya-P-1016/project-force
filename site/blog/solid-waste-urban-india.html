Solid waste programs for urban India: field lessons

Solid waste strategies that look elegant on paper often collapse on the second Monday — when residents miss segregation, trucks skip a lane, and the transfer station backs up. Here are the design decisions that decide whether a program survives contact with reality.

Characterize the actual waste, not the assumed waste

A ward in a Tier-1 CBD and a peri-urban ward in the same city generate entirely different waste compositions. Any program that uses a single tonnage assumption for both is already losing. Invest in a week of real sampling before you design the logistics plan.

Collection logistics: the 60/30/10 rule

In projects we've run across four states, about 60% of the delivery risk is in route design, 30% is in crew discipline, and 10% is in vehicle reliability. Most municipalities spend capex as if the ratio were reversed.

Design for the transfer station, not just the landfill

  • Plan capacity for peak day, not average — festival weeks and monsoon disruption double throughput.
  • Odour and leachate are political issues; a cheap transfer station costs you your next tender.
  • Separate fractions at the transfer stage, not only at the household stage.

Treat citizens as part of the system

The best cost-per-tonne performance we see is always in programs that treat the resident as an operator, not a subject. That means clear bin standards, a reliable schedule, published performance data, and a working grievance channel. Without the feedback loop, compliance decays within a quarter.

What success looks like at month 18

A functioning program, 18 months in, has segregated streams moving cleanly through defined routes, a transfer station operating within odour norms, a measurable reduction in informal dumping, and a cost-per-tonne that is trending down — not because corners are cut, but because the network is efficient.
